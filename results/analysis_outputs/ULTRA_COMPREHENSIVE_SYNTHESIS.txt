FRAMEWORK 1: REGRESSION MIXTURE MODELING
================================================================================

Purpose: Identify latent subgroups with different UCLA → EF vulnerability

Method:
-------
- Gaussian Mixture Models on augmented [predictors, outcome] space
- Tested K = 2, 3, 4, 5 components
- BIC model selection
- Stratified by gender to test moderation

Key Results:
------------
Outcome              Optimal K    Best Model         Interpretation
------------------------------------------------------------------------
WCST PE Rate         5            K=5 (BIC=38.5)     5 distinct classes
PRP Bottleneck       5            K=5 (BIC=54.0)     5 distinct classes
Stroop Interference  5            K=5 (BIC=89.4)     5 distinct classes

Class Characterization (WCST example):
- Class 1: Low UCLA, Low PE (N≈20, 22%) - Resilient
- Class 2: Moderate UCLA, Moderate PE (N≈35, 39%) - Typical
- Class 3: High UCLA, High PE (N≈15, 17%) - Vulnerable
- Class 4: Low UCLA, High PE (N≈10, 11%) - Non-lonely impaired
- Class 5: High UCLA, Low PE (N≈9, 10%) - Lonely resilient

Gender Differences:
- Males overrepresented in Class 3 (vulnerable: 62% male vs. 38% female)
- Females overrepresented in Class 5 (resilient: 78% female vs. 22% male)

Strengths:
----------
+ Identifies heterogeneous subpopulations (not just mean differences)
+ Data-driven clustering (no a priori assumptions about group structure)
+ Reveals "paradoxical" classes (lonely but high-functioning)

Limitations:
------------
- K=5 may be overfitting (small sample for 5 clusters)
- Class assignments unstable across bootstrap samples
- Does not test causal mechanisms

Publication Value: ★★★★☆
Interpretation: "One size does NOT fit all - loneliness impacts EF differently
                across distinct subpopulations, with males showing greater
                vulnerability in the 'high UCLA, high PE' profile."

FRAMEWORK 2: NORMATIVE MODELING
================================================================================

Purpose: Test if UCLA predicts deviations from age/gender/mood-expected EF

Method:
-------
- Build normative models using Gaussian Process regression
- Predictors: Age, Gender, DASS total (mood baseline)
- 10-fold cross-validation for unbiased deviation estimates
- Regress UCLA on z-scored deviations
- Test UCLA × Gender interaction

Key Results:
------------
Outcome              Normative R²    UCLA → Dev    UCLA×Gender    Interpretation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WCST PE Rate         0.998           β=-0.004      β=0.135        No UCLA effect
                                     p=.957        p=.174
PRP Bottleneck       0.953           β=0.108       β=-0.095       Marginal UCLA
                                     p=.187        p=.321         (trend only)
Stroop Interference  0.968           β=-0.001      β=-0.267       SIGNIFICANT
                                     p=.989        p=.044*        interaction

Stroop Interaction Breakdown:
- Females: UCLA → deviation, r = +0.15 (loneliness worsens Stroop)
- Males: UCLA → deviation, r = -0.12 (loneliness improves Stroop??)
- Difference significant at p=.044

Streng ths:
----------
+ Personalized deviation scores (individualized norms)
+ GP captures nonlinear age/gender/mood effects
+ Cross-validated (no overfitting)

Limitations:
------------
- Very high R² (>0.95) suggests potential overfitting despite CV
- GP may be "memorizing" the data (N=89 too small for GP flexibility)
- Stroop interaction counterintuitive (males show PROTECTIVE effect??)
- Does not align with other frameworks (WCST is usually the key outcome)

Publication Value: ★★☆☆☆
Interpretation: "Suspicious findings - extremely high R² and paradoxical gender
                effect in Stroop suggest model is overfitting. Normative modeling
                may require larger sample (N>200) for robust GP estimation."

CRITICAL WARNING:
-----------------
This framework's findings should be treated as EXPLORATORY ONLY. The R²=0.998
for WCST normative model is implausibly high and likely reflects overfitting.
The Stroop interaction (males showing PROTECTIVE loneliness effect) contradicts
all other frameworks and prior literature. Recommend not citing this framework
in publications without independent replication.

LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
================================================================================

CURRENT STUDY LIMITATIONS:
--------------------------
1. Cross-sectional design
   - Cannot establish temporal precedence (UCLA before EF impairment?)
   - Causal claims are tentative (model compatibility, not proof)
   - Need longitudinal data: T1 (UCLA) → T2 (DASS) → T3 (EF)

2. Sample size
   - N=89 adequate for GMM and simple Bayesian models
   - Insufficient for SEM (N>200 needed)
   - Insufficient for complex moderated mediation (divergences)
   - Power analysis: Recommend N=150 for replication

3. Measurement
   - Single timepoint for each construct
   - No behavioral measures of loneliness (only self-report)
   - EF tasks have low test-retest reliability (need multiple sessions)

4. Generalizability
   - South Korean university sample (WEIRD, homogeneous)
   - Age range restricted (20-50 years, mostly young adults)
   - Convenience sampling (online recruitment)

5. Unobserved confounders
   - Personality (neuroticism, extraversion)
   - SES, education quality
   - Sleep, diet, exercise
   - Medication use (SSRIs, stimulants)
